My MSc dissertation at Birmingham City University, in collaboration with ProBuild360, asked a question that turned out to be harder than I expected:

> Can multimodal deep learning beat strong tabular baselines for property price prediction in a constrained geographic market?

The short answer: yes, but not by as much as the literature implies, and the gains only show up once you handle three specific things.

## The baseline matters

A lot of multimodal property-prediction papers compare against weak baselines - linear regression, basic XGBoost with default hyperparameters. That is a low bar.

I started by tuning XGBoost on the structured features (postcode, EPC rating, tenure, build year, transaction history) until it stopped improving. R² of 0.74 on the test set. Most papers in this space report multimodal gains relative to baselines under 0.70. The headroom is smaller than it looks.

## Geo-spatial features need careful encoding

Postcodes have hierarchy: BCC vs B30 vs B30 1AA. Treating them as categorical labels throws away that hierarchy. Treating them as coordinates throws away the local-knowledge signal.

What worked: a two-stream encoder where one branch took embedded postcode districts (BCC, B1, B30 etc) and the other took (latitude, longitude) coordinates, combined with a small MLP. The combined embedding lifted R² by 0.04.

## Temporal data is mostly noise

The Birmingham market between 2018 and 2024 has so many regime shifts (Brexit, COVID, interest-rate cycles) that long-horizon temporal features mostly hurt the model. I ended up using only a 36-month rolling window of price-per-square-foot in the same postcode district. Anything longer added noise without adding signal.

## The fusion architecture

The architecture that won: structured tabular features through a tabular transformer (FT-Transformer), geo-spatial through the two-stream encoder, temporal through a short GRU, fused with a small attention head. Total parameters: about 4M. Trained in 40 minutes on a single A100.

## Final numbers

- XGBoost baseline: R² = 0.74, MAE = £28k
- Multimodal model: R² = 0.81, MAE = £21k

A 0.07 R² lift and a 25% reduction in MAE. Useful for production, but only because the baseline was already strong.

## What I would do differently

I would have started with the data audit, not the model. A week pulling outliers and mislabeled records would have moved both baselines and the final model more than any architectural choice.
